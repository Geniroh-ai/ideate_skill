# Speaker Notes — mySATHI × British Council Education India

Companion notes to `mysathi_british_council_deck.html`. Use these as the verbal layer that supports each slide — they are deliberately **not** printed on the slides themselves.

---

## Slide 01 — Title

> Frame the conversation as exploratory, not a sales pitch. Set up that we'll cover three questions in fifteen slides.

**Suggested opener:**
"Thanks for the time. We've put together a short set of slides — about fifteen — to share how we're thinking about a possible readiness layer that sits alongside BC Education's work in India. This is a working document, not a finished proposal. We'd value your reactions as we go."

---

## Slide 02 — Agenda

> Three questions structure the rest of the deck. Anchor expectations now so the audience knows where we're heading.

- The gap, and why it matters to BC Education
- How mySATHI works and where it could plug in
- What a light-touch 3–6 month pilot could look like

---

## Slide 03 — The macro

> The structural gap.
> Universities, exam bodies and corporate clients today see marks-based signals — proxies for memorisation, not for the 4Cs that drive success in UK classrooms and workplaces. A capability-based readiness layer doesn't yet exist at scale in India.

**Talking points:**
- 759K Indian students studied abroad in 2024 — the volume isn't the problem
- ~98.9K of them came to the UK — a meaningful but contestable share
- Pass rates on global qualifications (CFA Level I ~45% globally) underline how hard the assessments BC delivers actually are
- Vision 2035 puts education at the centre — the timing for a readiness layer is unusually good

---

## Slide 04 — BC Education today

> Position BC's three product lines neutrally and respectfully. The point is not to critique, it is to surface the *common upstream constraint*.

**Frame:**
"All three product lines — exams, training, mobility-adjacent work — share the same dependency: candidate readiness *before* the exam, programme or application begins. That's the only place we think a readiness layer earns its place."

---

## Slide 05 — The diagnostic gap

> The strategic implication.
> A readiness layer doesn't compete with BC Education's portfolio — it sits alongside, and could make existing product lines incrementally stronger.

**Watch-outs:**
- Don't overclaim "BC has a problem". Phrase it as *"the structural reality of the market today"*
- The "what it costs" column is observational, not accusatory
- If pushed on numbers, acknowledge the figures are directional and offer to share methodology

---

## Slide 06 — Section divider: The Platform

> Brief pause slide. Use it to land the framing: *"What we are about to describe is one diagnostic engine, applied across three contexts."*

---

## Slide 07 — mySATHI in one slide

> Avoid jargon ("IRT", "psychometrics") unless invited. Lead with what the platform *produces* for a B2B partner, then return to how it works under the hood if asked.

**Two-sentence version:**
"mySATHI is a computer-adaptive readiness assessment built around the 4Cs. Every candidate gets a single scaled Readiness Index, four sub-scores, and a longitudinal track."

---

## Slide 08 — The 4Cs

> Frame the 4Cs as *constructs*, not slogans. We measure them; we don't ask people to self-report.

**If asked "why these four?":**
- They map cleanly to NEP 2020 capability framing
- They are the capabilities UK universities and global employers actually select on
- Each is operationalised into measurable item types — not mood-board language

## Slide 11 — Use Case A: Pre-exam readiness

> If BC pushes back on "another step in the funnel", emphasise that the screen is *optional*, can be paid or free, and can be positioned as a service to candidates rather than a hurdle.

**Anticipated questions:**
- "Won't this reduce registrations?" → Counter-evidence: candidates value diagnostic clarity; can be positioned as a "readiness check" not a gate
- "Who pays?" → Multiple commercial models possible — happy to explore

---

## Slide 12 — Use Case B: Training

> The shift from "hours delivered" to "capability uplift" is the real story. Spend extra time here if the audience is buyer-facing.

> For the buyer (HR head, university dean):
> Capability uplift can be reported in scaled, comparable terms — for example, "a cohort moving from a Communication score of 540 to 680 in 12 weeks" — alongside the qualitative outcomes BC already shares. That is a stronger procurement story than seat-time alone.

---

## Slide 13 — Use Case C: Mobility & scholarships

> Strategic note for BC.
> Sits naturally alongside the UK–India Vision 2035 agenda and the UK Education Hub launched February 2026 — moving the corridor incrementally from access-led to capability-led.

**Equity angle (only if it lands):**
- Capability is less correlated with school type and family income than marks-only signals
- A capability layer can surface high-potential Tier 2/3 candidates that traditional filters miss
- This is genuine — flag it confidently if BC values it

---

## Slide 14 — Why mySATHI

> Don't read the four columns. Pick the two that matter most for the audience in the room and let the slide do the rest.

- Pan-India reach — the cities BC's exam-board partners care most about
- Proctored network — already meets exam-integrity standards
- IRT-based engine — defensible scaled scores
- Longitudinal tracking — a unique data asset over time
